# Programmable Money

A dollar in a bank account can be sent or received. A dollar in a smart contract can be programmed — conditioned on time, events, multi-party approval, or arbitrary logic. This is not a marginal improvement. It changes what money can do.

Traditional payment systems treat money as a static balance that moves between accounts through instructions. Onchain, money and logic coexist in the same execution environment. The payment itself can enforce its own terms.

## Conditional Payments

A simple example: an escrow contract holds payment until both parties confirm delivery. The buyer deposits funds. The seller ships the goods. When the buyer confirms receipt — or when an oracle confirms delivery tracking data — the contract releases payment to the seller. If neither condition is met within 30 days, the buyer gets a refund.

Traditional escrow services do this but are slow, expensive, and require trusting the escrow agent. A smart contract escrow is transparent (both parties read the code), automatic (no human in the loop), and cheap (deployed once, reused indefinitely).

Conditional logic extends to any trigger verifiable onchain or through an oracle: milestone completion, passage of time, a price threshold, a third-party attestation, or a DAO vote.

## Streaming Payments

Sablier and other streaming protocols enable continuous, per-second payment flows. An employer deposits a month's salary into a streaming contract. The employee accrues earnings every second and can withdraw their accumulated balance at any time. If the employment ends, the employer cancels the stream and recovers unstreamed funds.

This model is more honest about how compensation works. Employees earn money continuously, not in arbitrary biweekly or monthly chunks. It also improves cash flow management — the employer's funds are committed but not fully disbursed, and the employee has real-time access to earned wages.

Streaming payments are already used for DAO contributor compensation, service agreements, and vesting schedules. The infrastructure is proven. Extending it to conventional payroll requires the wallet and banking integrations that account abstraction and fiat on/off-ramps provide.

## Programmable Compliance

Payment rules can be embedded in the token or the wallet, not just in the application layer.

A regulated stablecoin can enforce transfer restrictions at the contract level: only wallets that have completed KYC verification (attested onchain through a credential) can hold the token. Sanctioned addresses can be blocked by the contract itself, not by a centralized compliance team reviewing transactions after the fact.

A corporate treasury wallet can require multi-signature approval for transfers above a threshold, restrict payments to a pre-approved vendor list, and log every transaction to an onchain audit trail — all enforced by code, not by policy documents and manual reviews.

This is a structural upgrade. Compliance in traditional finance is detective — violations are identified after they occur, through audits and monitoring. Onchain compliance can be preventive — invalid transactions revert before they execute.

## Composability with DeFi

Onchain money doesn't just move. It can work.

A business receives a $100,000 USDC payment. A smart contract automatically deposits $80,000 into a lending protocol, allocates $15,000 to an operating expense wallet, and routes $5,000 to a tax reserve. One transaction, triggered by the incoming payment.

Treasury management for onchain-native businesses already works this way. Idle stablecoin balances earn 3-5% APY through lending protocols like Aave and Compound — the onchain equivalent of a money market fund, backed by overcollateralized lending.

Payment channels can integrate with decentralized exchanges to handle currency conversion. A sender pays in USDC. The recipient wants EURC (euro-denominated stablecoin). A DEX swap executes atomically within the payment transaction. No FX broker. No spread markup beyond the DEX fee (typically 0.01-0.05% for stablecoin pairs).

## Subscriptions and Recurring Payments

Traditional recurring payments require the payer to grant a merchant ongoing access to their payment method. The merchant initiates charges. The payer's recourse is to dispute charges after the fact or cancel through their bank.

Onchain subscriptions invert this. A smart contract holds the subscriber's authorization: the merchant can pull up to $X per month. The subscriber revokes at any time with a single transaction. The contract enforces the cap — the merchant cannot overcharge.

Session keys (from account abstraction) make this practical. A subscription service receives a session key permitting a fixed monthly USDC transfer. The key cannot be used for anything else. It expires automatically.

## What This Adds Up To

Programmable money is not one feature. It is a design space. Every business process involving payments — invoicing, payroll, treasury management, lending, insurance payouts, subscription billing — can be expressed as a smart contract that executes automatically and without intermediaries.

The building blocks are live: stablecoins for value, L2s for cost and speed, account abstraction for UX, composable DeFi for yield and conversion. What remains is integration with the systems where businesses and consumers already operate.
