# Account Abstraction

Ethereum wallets were designed for cryptographers. You generate a private key, derive an address, sign transactions with ECDSA, and pay gas in ETH. Lose the key and you lose access. Every interaction assumes the user understands signatures, networks, and gas.

Account abstraction replaces this model with wallets that behave more like normal software accounts. On Ethereum today, two important building blocks are ERC-4337 and EIP-7702. They solve different problems and should not be conflated.

## ERC-4337: Smart Accounts Without Protocol Changes

ERC-4337, live since March 2023, introduces smart accounts without a consensus-layer change. Instead of submitting ordinary transactions directly to the public mempool, users submit `UserOperation` objects to an alternative mempool. Bundlers collect them and call an onchain `EntryPoint` contract, which asks the user's smart account and optional paymaster to validate and execute the action.

This indirection enables several capabilities that plain externally owned accounts do not support cleanly:

**Gas sponsorship.** A paymaster contract can pay gas on behalf of the user. A payment app can hide ETH entirely and recover its economics elsewhere in the product.

**Flexible authentication.** Smart accounts can validate more than a plain ECDSA signature. That makes passkey-based UX possible, but passkeys come from wallet implementations and WebAuthn flows, not from ERC-4337 alone.

**Batched execution.** A single user action can execute multiple contract calls atomically. That removes multi-step approval flows that are awkward in mainstream payment UX.

**Programmable validation.** Spending limits, multisig policies, recovery logic, timelocks, and merchant-specific permissions can be enforced at the wallet layer.

## EIP-7702: Adding Smart-Account Behavior to EOAs

EIP-7702, activated in Ethereum's Pectra upgrade on May 7, 2025, takes a different approach. It adds a transaction type that lets an externally owned account set a delegation pointer to contract code. That delegation is persistent until the account changes or clears it. In other words, 7702 is not a one-transaction trick; it is a protocol primitive for letting EOAs execute through contract code while remaining transaction-originating accounts.

This matters because huge amounts of value still sit in EOAs. ERC-4337 often implies moving users into dedicated smart accounts. EIP-7702 lets existing wallets gain some smart-account behavior without moving assets to a new address.

The two standards are complementary. ERC-4337 is a wallet infrastructure pattern centered on smart accounts, bundlers, and paymasters. EIP-7702 is a protocol feature that lets EOAs delegate execution to shared contract logic. Wallets can combine them, but one does not replace the other.

## Why This Matters for Payments

**Session keys and spend permissions** allow a wallet to grant temporary, scoped permissions to an application. A user could authorize a payment app to spend up to $500 per day for the next 30 days without approving each transfer individually. That behavior comes from wallet logic rather than a single universal Ethereum standard, but it is one of the clearest account-abstraction payment unlocks.

This is the UX model that subscriptions, recurring payments, and low-friction commerce require. A confirmation popup for every payment is not workable at scale.

**Spending controls** can be enforced by the wallet itself. A parent could configure a child's wallet with a daily cap and merchant restrictions. A treasury wallet could require multiple signers above a threshold. Those rules live in code rather than in a policy PDF or a bank portal.

**Recovery without seed phrases** is also possible. Guardians, passkeys, hardware devices, and recovery policies can be composed into safer flows than a single recovery phrase written on paper.

## Adoption Status

As of early 2026, smart-account infrastructure is common across major L2 ecosystems. Coinbase Smart Wallet, Safe's account stack, Circle Wallets, Alchemy, ZeroDev, and Biconomy have all pushed the model forward. The pattern is real, but adoption is still uneven across wallets, apps, and regions.

Gas sponsorship is one of the earliest and most visible features to ship. Passkey onboarding is also growing, especially in embedded wallets and mobile-first apps. What is not solved yet is universal portability: features such as session keys, recovery methods, and spending policies still vary significantly by wallet implementation.

The infrastructure is credible. The remaining challenge is productization in payment contexts: subscriptions, point-of-sale, invoicing, payroll, and merchant tooling that feel familiar to non-crypto users.
