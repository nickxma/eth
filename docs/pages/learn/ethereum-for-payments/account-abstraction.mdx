# Account Abstraction

Ethereum wallets were designed for cryptographers. You generate a private key, derive an address, sign transactions with ECDSA, and pay gas in ETH. Lose the key and you lose everything. Send to the wrong address and there's no recourse. Every interaction requires ETH in your wallet before you can do anything, even if you're transacting in stablecoins.

Account abstraction replaces this model with smart contract wallets that behave like normal software accounts. Two standards make this possible: ERC-4337 and EIP-7702.

## ERC-4337: Smart Accounts Without Protocol Changes

ERC-4337, live on Ethereum since March 2023, introduces smart contract wallets through an alternative transaction pipeline. Instead of submitting transactions directly to the mempool, users submit "UserOperations" to a separate mempool. Bundlers collect these operations and submit them to an onchain EntryPoint contract, which calls the user's smart contract wallet to validate and execute the operation.

This indirection enables several capabilities that externally owned accounts (EOAs) cannot support:

**Gas sponsorship.** A paymaster contract can pay the gas fee on behalf of the user. A stablecoin payment app can sponsor all gas costs so users never need to hold or understand ETH. The paymaster might be funded by the application, by the merchant, or by deducting a fee from the stablecoin transfer itself. From the user's perspective, gas does not exist.

**Arbitrary signature verification.** Smart contract wallets can validate any signature scheme, not just ECDSA. This enables passkey authentication — users sign transactions with their device's biometric sensor (Face ID, fingerprint) through the WebAuthn standard. No seed phrase. No browser extension. The private key lives in the device's secure enclave and never leaves it.

**Batched execution.** A single UserOperation can execute multiple calls atomically. Transfer stablecoins, approve a subscription, and update a registry — one confirmation, one signature. This removes the multi-step approval flows that frustrate users in current DeFi interfaces.

**Modular validation logic.** The wallet's validation function is programmable. This opens the design space for spending limits, timelocks, multi-signature requirements, and social recovery — all enforced at the wallet level, not by a custodian.

## EIP-7702: Upgrading Existing Accounts

EIP-7702, included in Ethereum's Pectra upgrade in 2025, takes a different approach. It allows existing EOAs to temporarily delegate their execution to a smart contract within a single transaction. The EOA sets a "delegation designator" pointing to a contract's code, which then governs how that transaction executes.

This matters because billions of dollars in assets sit in existing EOAs. ERC-4337 requires users to migrate to new smart contract wallets. EIP-7702 lets existing wallets gain smart account features without moving assets. An EOA can opt into gas sponsorship, batched execution, or custom validation for individual transactions while remaining a normal EOA otherwise.

The two standards are complementary. ERC-4337 is better for new users who start with smart accounts from day one. EIP-7702 is better for existing users and applications that need backward compatibility.

## Why This Matters for Payments

**Session keys** allow a wallet to grant temporary, scoped permissions to an application. A user could authorize a payment app to spend up to $500 per day from their wallet for the next 30 days — without approving each individual transaction. The session key enforces the constraints onchain. If the app tries to exceed the limit, the transaction reverts.

This is the UX model that recurring payments, subscriptions, and tap-to-pay interactions require. Today's wallet confirmation popup for every transaction is incompatible with how people actually pay for things.

**Spending limits and controls** can be enforced by the wallet contract itself. A parent could configure a child's wallet with a $50 daily limit and restrict it to approved merchants. A corporate treasury could require two of three signers for transfers above $10,000. These rules execute onchain — no custodian or admin panel can override them.

**Recovery without seed phrases** is possible through social recovery schemes. A user designates three trusted contacts (or institutions). If they lose access, two of three guardians can authorize a key rotation. This is meaningfully safer than a 12-word phrase written on paper and stored in a drawer.

## Adoption Status

As of early 2026, smart accounts are deployed across most major L2 networks. Coinbase's smart wallet on Base, Safe's modular accounts, and Biconomy's SDK are among the most widely integrated. The majority of new wallet deployments on high-activity L2s are smart contract wallets rather than EOAs.

Gas sponsorship is the most adopted feature. Most consumer-facing applications on L2s now sponsor gas entirely, making the ETH fee invisible to users. Passkey-based authentication is growing but still early — limited primarily to mobile-native wallets and newer applications.

The infrastructure works. The remaining challenge is adoption across payment-specific applications: point-of-sale systems, invoicing platforms, and payroll services that integrate these capabilities into familiar workflows.
