# Stablecoins on Ethereum

Stablecoins are the most widely used application on Ethereum. Their combined market capitalization exceeded $200 billion by early 2026, with the majority issued natively on Ethereum or bridged to Ethereum L2s. They serve as the unit of account for onchain payments — the thing people actually want to send and receive.

## What Stablecoins Are

A stablecoin is a token whose value is pegged to a reference asset, usually the U.S. dollar. The mechanism for maintaining the peg varies by design, but the result is the same: a digital dollar that moves on blockchain rails.

For payments, this solves the obvious problem with using ETH or BTC as money. No merchant wants to accept a currency that moves 5% in an afternoon. Stablecoins separate the settlement network (Ethereum) from the unit of account (dollars), giving users blockchain's speed and programmability without its volatility.

## Issuance Models

**Fiat-backed (custodial).** USDC (Circle) and USDT (Tether) are backed by reserves held at regulated financial institutions — primarily U.S. Treasuries, bank deposits, and money market instruments. Each token is redeemable 1:1 for dollars through the issuer. USDT's circulating supply exceeds $140 billion; USDC exceeds $55 billion.

The trust assumption is explicit: you trust the issuer to maintain reserves and honor redemptions. Circle publishes monthly reserve attestations. Tether has faced scrutiny over reserve transparency, though it has published quarterly reports since 2023.

**Fiat-backed (institutional).** PYUSD (PayPal) represents a newer class of stablecoins issued by regulated financial institutions rather than crypto-native companies. PYUSD is backed by dollar deposits, U.S. Treasuries, and money market funds, with reserves held at regulated custodians. Its integration into PayPal's existing payment network gives it a distribution channel that crypto-native issuers lack.

**Crypto-collateralized (decentralized).** DAI and its successor USDS (Sky, formerly MakerDAO) are minted by depositing crypto collateral — primarily ETH and USDC — into smart contract vaults. The system is overcollateralized: minting $100 of DAI requires $150+ of collateral. Liquidation mechanisms protect the peg if collateral values drop. No single entity can freeze or blacklist DAI, making it attractive for users who value censorship resistance.

**Algorithmic.** Some stablecoins attempted to maintain pegs through algorithms and incentive mechanisms without full collateral backing. Terra/UST's collapse in May 2022 — erasing $40 billion in value — demonstrated the fragility of this approach. Algorithmic stablecoins are largely discredited for payments use cases.

## Why Ethereum Dominates Stablecoin Issuance

Ethereum is the primary issuance chain for USDC, USDT, DAI, and PYUSD. Several factors explain this:

Network effects are self-reinforcing. Liquidity attracts applications, applications attract users, users attract liquidity. Ethereum had the earliest stablecoin adoption and has maintained its position through this cycle.

DeFi composability gives Ethereum-native stablecoins utility beyond simple transfers. USDC can be deposited into lending protocols, used as collateral, traded on DEXs, or routed through payment channels — all without leaving the ecosystem.

Institutional trust matters. Treasurers and compliance teams evaluate validator decentralization, track record, and audit history. Ethereum's 10-year operating history and broad validator set provide assurance that newer chains lack.

L2 networks extend Ethereum's stablecoin ecosystem to low-cost environments. USDC on Base or Arbitrum costs fractions of a cent to transfer while inheriting Ethereum's security model. Circle's Cross-Chain Transfer Protocol (CCTP) enables native USDC minting on L2s, avoiding the liquidity fragmentation that plagues bridged assets.

## Regulatory Landscape

The EU's Markets in Crypto-Assets (MiCA) regulation, effective mid-2024, classifies stablecoins as either "e-money tokens" (pegged to a single currency) or "asset-referenced tokens" (pegged to a basket). Issuers must hold reserves in EU-regulated banks, obtain authorization, and meet capital requirements. Circle obtained an e-money license in France. Tether has faced compliance challenges under MiCA's requirements.

In the U.S., Congress has debated multiple stablecoin bills. Key provisions across proposals include requiring 1:1 reserve backing, restricting issuance to regulated entities, and establishing federal or state oversight. As of early 2026, comprehensive legislation has not passed, though state-level frameworks (notably New York's BitLicense) govern some issuers.

Regulatory clarity is broadly expected to accelerate adoption. Most major issuers have adopted compliance-forward positions — KYC for large redemptions, sanctions screening, and the ability to freeze addresses by court order. Whether that last capability is a feature or a vulnerability depends on the use case.

## Stablecoins as Payment Infrastructure

Daily stablecoin transfer volume routinely exceeds $30 billion. Much of this is settlement between exchanges and market makers, but the share attributable to real-world payments — payroll, invoicing, remittances, B2B settlement — is growing.

The unit economics are compelling. Sending $10,000 in USDC on Base costs less than $0.01 and settles in under two seconds. The same transfer via international wire costs $25-50 and takes 1-3 business days. For a business making hundreds of cross-border payments monthly, the difference is material.

Stablecoins are the payment layer. Ethereum is the settlement layer. The combination gives payments a programmable, always-on, globally accessible foundation that traditional rails were not designed to provide.
