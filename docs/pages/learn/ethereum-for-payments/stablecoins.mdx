# Stablecoins on Ethereum

Stablecoins are the most widely used payments-related asset class in crypto. By early 2026, global stablecoin market capitalization was above $300 billion. Not all of that activity lives on Ethereum, but Ethereum and its rollups remain the center of gravity for regulated dollar tokens, DeFi liquidity, and institutional onchain settlement.

## What Stablecoins Are

A stablecoin is a token designed to track a reference asset, usually the U.S. dollar. The mechanism varies by design, but the practical goal is the same: a digital dollar that moves on blockchain rails.

For payments, this matters because it separates the settlement network from the unit of account. Users get blockchain speed and programmability without taking the price volatility of ETH or BTC.

## Issuance Models

**Fiat-backed (custodial).** USDC and USDT are backed by reserve assets such as short-dated Treasuries, cash, and cash equivalents. The trust assumption is explicit: users depend on the issuer and its banking partners to maintain reserves and honor redemptions. Direct redemption is usually available to approved customers or institutional counterparties, not frictionlessly to every wallet holder.

**Fiat-backed (institutional).** PYUSD represents a newer class of payment-oriented stablecoin distributed through a large incumbent payments brand. It is issued by Paxos for PayPal and backed by dollar deposits, Treasuries, and similar cash equivalents. Its strategic advantage is distribution, not decentralization.

**Crypto-collateralized (decentralized).** DAI and USDS within the Sky ecosystem are backed by onchain collateral and governance-managed risk parameters rather than a single bank account. They are generally overcollateralized, but they are not trustless in an absolute sense: governance, collateral composition, oracle design, and emergency powers all matter. DAI also remains live; USDS did not simply replace it overnight.

**Algorithmic.** Stablecoins that try to hold a peg mainly through incentives and reflexive market structure have a poor track record. Terra/UST's collapse in 2022 remains the clearest example of why undercollateralized algorithmic designs are a bad fit for payment infrastructure.

## Why Ethereum Matters for Stablecoins

Ethereum is not the only important stablecoin chain. Tron, for example, carries a very large share of USDT supply and retail transfer activity. But Ethereum still matters disproportionately for several reasons:

Network effects are self-reinforcing. Liquidity attracts applications, applications attract users, and users attract more liquidity.

DeFi composability gives Ethereum-native stablecoins utility beyond simple transfers. They can be used in lending markets, DEXs, custody platforms, and payment workflows without leaving the broader ecosystem.

Institutional trust matters. Treasurers and compliance teams care about validator decentralization, operational history, client diversity, and the depth of surrounding infrastructure. Ethereum scores better than most alternatives on those dimensions.

L2 networks extend Ethereum's stablecoin ecosystem into cheaper environments. Base, Arbitrum, and OP Mainnet support low-fee transfers, but the security model is still shaped by sequencer design, proof systems, and governance controls. Circle's Cross-Chain Transfer Protocol helps by burning and minting supported USDC across chains instead of relying on wrapped bridge assets.

## Regulatory Landscape

The EU's Markets in Crypto-Assets regulation classifies fiat-pegged stablecoins as e-money tokens or asset-referenced tokens depending on structure and reference asset. It imposes authorization, reserve, disclosure, and redemption requirements. Circle obtained an electronic money institution license in France and issues MiCA-compliant USDC and EURC in the EEA. Not every global stablecoin issuer has taken the same path.

In the United States, the GENIUS Act was enacted on July 18, 2025. It establishes a federal framework for payment stablecoins, including reserve, disclosure, supervision, and issuer eligibility requirements. As of March 2026, the operational details are still being implemented by regulators and market participants.

Regulatory clarity should help adoption, but it does not erase the core tradeoff. Most large payment stablecoins remain centrally issued, censorable, and legally tied to regulated intermediaries.

## Stablecoins as Payment Infrastructure

Stablecoin transfer volume is enormous, but raw transfer volume should not be treated as the same thing as economic payment volume. Exchange flows, market-making, and automated activity inflate the headline numbers. Even after adjusting for that, the real-payment share is clearly growing across treasury, B2B settlement, remittances, payroll, and commerce.

The unit economics can still be compelling. Sending a large USDC transfer on a major L2 often costs a fraction of a cent to a few cents in network fees and appears to the recipient almost immediately. The same transfer over cross-border bank rails can involve fixed fees, FX spreads, cutoffs, and multi-day settlement windows. The real cost comparison, however, has to include off-ramp fees, compliance overhead, and treasury operations, not just gas.

Stablecoins are the payment asset. Ethereum is one of the main settlement environments. Together they provide a programmable, always-on, globally accessible foundation that legacy rails were not designed to offer.
