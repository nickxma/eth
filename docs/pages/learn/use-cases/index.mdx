# Stablecoin Use Cases

Stablecoin transfer volume ran into the tens of trillions of dollars in 2024, but that headline figure should not be read as pure payment volume. It includes trading, bot flows, treasury rebalancing, and other non-payment activity. The more interesting question is narrower: where do stablecoins already solve real payment problems better than legacy rails?

## Where Traditional Rails Break

International wire transfers often take 1-5 business days and can involve fixed fees, FX spreads, and opaque correspondent-bank routing. Small-value cross-border transfers are worse: according to the World Bank, the global average cost of sending $200 remained about 6.5% in 2025, with some corridors materially higher.

Domestic rails are better in some markets, but still constrained. Card networks impose meaningful merchant fees. ACH is cheap but not real time by default. Faster-payment systems improve speed but usually remain domestic and are not natively programmable.

These are not accidental inefficiencies. They are consequences of a system built on message passing between institutions that maintain separate ledgers and reconcile asynchronously.

## What Stablecoins Change

A stablecoin transfer is a state update on a shared ledger. Settlement is closer to the payment event itself rather than a later reconciliation step. This removes parts of the intermediary chain and compresses the timeline from days to seconds for user-visible confirmation.

On Ethereum L2s, transaction fees are often fractions of a cent to a few cents and user-visible confirmation usually arrives in seconds. Finality is more nuanced than that sentence suggests, but the user experience is still dramatically faster than correspondent banking. The sender and receiver can be anywhere. No SWIFT messages. No nostro/vostro account chain.

This makes several categories of payment newly viable or materially cheaper:

- **Remittances** — Workers sending money home can sometimes avoid the fee burden of traditional remittance rails.
- **Global payouts** — Companies paying contractors and sellers across borders can settle much faster.
- **Payroll** — Net-pay delivery can be routed onchain even if payroll processing remains fiat-based.
- **Embedded finance** — Platforms can build escrow, instant payout, and programmable settlement into their products.
- **Microtransactions** — Small-value payments become more viable when transfer costs fall sharply.
- **Agentic commerce** — Software agents can use programmable wallets and constrained spending policies.

## Current Adoption

Stablecoin supply moved above $300 billion by early 2026. USDT remains the largest token by circulation and is especially important on Tron and in emerging-market retail flows. USDC is smaller by supply but disproportionately important in regulated, institutional, and Ethereum-centric workflows.

Geographic adoption is uneven and revealing. In countries with stable currencies and functional banking, stablecoins are still mostly used by crypto-native users, fintechs, and institutions. In countries facing inflation, capital controls, weak banking interoperability, or expensive cross-border rails, they increasingly function as practical financial infrastructure.

## What's Next

Regulatory clarity is accelerating. The EU's MiCA framework is in force. The United States enacted the GENIUS Act in July 2025, though implementation remains ongoing. As rules become clearer, the barrier for institutional adoption drops, but compliance still shapes which use cases can scale cleanly.

The convergence of cheaper rollups, more usable wallets, and more explicit stablecoin rules suggests that stablecoins are moving from crypto-native infrastructure toward broader payment plumbing. The pages in this section cover the places where that shift is most visible today.
