# Stablecoin Use Cases

Stablecoins settled over $27 trillion in onchain transaction volume in 2024 — more than Visa's annual payment volume. Most of that was trading and DeFi activity. The more interesting question is what's happening at the edges: the non-speculative use cases where stablecoins solve problems that existing payment infrastructure cannot.

## Where Traditional Rails Break

International wire transfers take 1-5 business days and cost $25-50 per transaction. Correspondent banking chains add friction and opacity — a payment from Nigeria to the Philippines might pass through three intermediary banks, each taking a cut and adding delay. Small-value cross-border transfers are even worse: remittance corridors average 6.4% in fees globally, with some African corridors exceeding 10%.

Domestic payments are faster but still carry structural costs. Card networks charge merchants 1.5-3.5% per transaction. ACH is cheap but slow. Real-time payment systems like FedNow exist but lack programmability and have limited international reach.

These aren't temporary inefficiencies. They're features of a system built on message-passing between ledgers that don't share state. Every intermediary in the chain maintains its own ledger, reconciles asynchronously, and prices risk accordingly.

## What Stablecoins Change

A stablecoin transfer is a ledger update on a shared, global database. Settlement is the transaction itself — there's no second step. This eliminates the intermediary chain and collapses the settlement window from days to seconds.

On Ethereum L2s, transaction fees are typically under $0.01. Transfers are final within minutes. The sender and receiver can be anywhere. No correspondent banking relationship required. No SWIFT messages. No nostro/vostro account management.

This makes several categories of payment newly viable or dramatically cheaper:

- **Remittances** — Workers sending money home can avoid 6%+ fees on sub-$500 transfers.
- **Global payouts** — Companies paying international contractors can settle in minutes instead of waiting on wire transfers.
- **Payroll** — Recurring salary payments can be programmed and executed onchain with precise timing.
- **Embedded finance** — Platforms can integrate payment flows without building on top of legacy card networks.
- **Microtransactions** — Sub-cent payments become economically feasible when transaction fees drop below $0.01.
- **Agentic commerce** — Software agents can hold and transfer value autonomously using programmable money.

## Current Adoption

Stablecoin supply crossed $210 billion in early 2025. USDT dominates with roughly 60% market share, primarily on Tron and Ethereum. USDC holds about 25%, with stronger adoption among regulated institutions and developers building on Ethereum and its L2s.

Geographic adoption is uneven and revealing. In countries with stable currencies and functional banking — the US, EU, Japan — stablecoins are mostly used by crypto-native users and institutions. In countries with currency instability, capital controls, or underdeveloped banking — Argentina, Turkey, Nigeria, the Philippines — stablecoins serve as practical financial infrastructure. An estimated 28% of crypto transactions in sub-Saharan Africa are stablecoin transfers, many of which are remittances or merchant payments.

## What's Next

Regulatory clarity is accelerating. The EU's MiCA framework established stablecoin rules in 2024. The US is moving toward federal stablecoin legislation. As regulatory frameworks solidify, the barrier for institutional adoption drops.

The convergence of L2 scaling (sub-cent fees, sub-second finality), regulatory progress, and growing real-world adoption suggests stablecoins are transitioning from crypto-native infrastructure to general-purpose payment rails. The use cases covered in this section are where that transition is most visible today.
