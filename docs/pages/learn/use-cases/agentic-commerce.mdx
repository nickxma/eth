# Agentic Commerce

Software agents are beginning to spend money, but most current systems are still tightly supervised. An AI assistant might pay for an API, provision compute, or execute a crypto transaction on behalf of a user. Today this often happens through brittle browser automation or tightly scoped wallet tooling rather than through a mature agent-native payments stack.

Programmable money on programmable rails changes what is possible. An agent with a stablecoin wallet and constrained smart-account permissions can transact directly without pretending to be a human clicking through checkout pages.

## Why Agents Need New Payment Rails

Credit cards are designed around human cardholders, fraud models, and merchant flows built for people. A software agent making purchases can look anomalous or outright suspicious to those systems.

Bank transfers are also a bad fit. They require pre-existing relationships, banking hours, and authorization patterns that do not map cleanly onto autonomous software.

Stablecoin wallets remove some of these constraints. A wallet is a key or a smart-account policy, so software can hold and transfer funds directly. But regulated counterparties may still require identity, screening, limits, and custody controls. The protocol is permissionless; commerce often is not.

## The Emerging Agent Payment Stack

The stack is taking shape across several layers:

**Wallet infrastructure.** Agent wallets are typically smart-account wallets with programmable spending controls. A user funds a wallet for the agent with rules such as per-transaction caps, daily limits, and whitelisted counterparties. The agent then operates inside those bounds.

**Service discovery.** For agents to buy services from other agents or automated providers, they need machine-readable pricing, payment instructions, and service descriptions.

**Payment protocols.** Simple transfers are enough for one-off purchases. Metered usage, subscriptions, and delivery-versus-payment flows usually need escrow, prepaid balances, or streaming and settlement logic.

## Agent-to-Agent Transactions

The novel part of agentic commerce is agents transacting with each other. A research agent needs data. A data-provider agent has it. They negotiate a price, exchange the service, and settle programmatically.

That requires:

1. A common medium of exchange.
2. A settlement layer both parties can access.
3. A way to make payment conditional on delivery.

Early implementations are simple: one agent escrows funds, the counterparty delivers a machine-verifiable result, and the contract releases payment. The difficult part is dispute resolution when delivery quality is hard to verify automatically.

## Current Experiments

**Skyfire** is building payment and identity infrastructure specifically for AI agents. **Coinbase AgentKit** gives developers a well-supported path to fund agent wallets and let LLM-based systems perform onchain actions. These are still early systems, but they demonstrate the core pattern.

Tool-use frameworks from model providers also matter because they let agents call wallet functions safely and deterministically. The important shift is not that a model "knows crypto"; it is that the model can invoke a payment tool with explicit guardrails.

These are early-stage systems. Transaction volumes remain small. But they demonstrate the core pattern: software can hold, evaluate, and transfer value within machine-enforceable limits.

## Guardrails and Control

Autonomous spending raises obvious questions, so the control surface matters:

- **Spending limits.** Wallet logic can enforce per-transaction, daily, and monthly caps.
- **Whitelists.** Agents can be limited to approved contracts or counterparties.
- **Multisig approval.** Higher-value actions can still require human approval.
- **Kill switches.** Users can revoke the agent's authority by changing wallet permissions.

These controls live at the wallet and contract layer rather than in the model's reasoning loop. That is important because it makes the financial constraints harder for the agent to bypass accidentally or maliciously.

## What This Means

Agentic commerce sits at the intersection of AI capability and programmable money. As agents become more capable at planning and tool use, their need for a native way to hold and transfer value grows.

The timeline is uncertain. Widespread agent-to-agent commerce still depends on reliability, standards, and trust. But the payment rail itself is no longer the main bottleneck.
