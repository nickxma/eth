# Remittances

Officially recorded remittance flows to low- and middle-income countries reached an estimated $685 billion in 2024, according to the World Bank. The global average cost of sending $200 remained about 6.5%, far above the UN sustainable-development target of 3%. In many sub-Saharan African corridors, the cost is materially higher.

These costs are not mysterious. They are the price of moving money through a chain of intermediaries: the sending agent, the sending bank, one or more correspondent banks, the receiving bank, and often a cash-out network. Each layer adds margin, delay, or both.

## How Stablecoin Remittances Work

The sender converts local currency into a stablecoin such as USDC or USDT through an exchange, wallet app, or local broker. The stablecoin is transferred directly to the recipient's wallet. The recipient can then hold the stablecoin or convert it into local currency through a local exchange, fintech, or cash-out partner.

The transfer leg is usually cheap on L2 networks such as Base, Arbitrum, or OP Mainnet. The real cost to the user sits at the edges: converting between local fiat and stablecoins. In liquid corridors, those conversion costs can still be meaningfully lower than traditional remittance pricing. In illiquid or restricted corridors, the edge costs can absorb most of the savings.

## Corridor-Specific Adoption

**US to Latin America.** This is the world's largest remittance region. Mexico alone received about $68 billion in remittances in 2024. In Argentina and other high-inflation markets, recipients often prefer to hold dollar stablecoins rather than convert immediately into local currency, so the stablecoin functions as both a remittance rail and a savings vehicle.

**Africa.** Intra-African remittances are notoriously expensive, and banking connectivity across the continent remains fragmented. Mobile-money-to-crypto bridges are emerging. Yellow Card, for example, operates across 20 African countries and focuses on stablecoin on-ramp and off-ramp infrastructure tied to local payment methods.

**Southeast Asia.** The Philippines received about $40 billion in remittances in 2024. Platforms such as Coins.ph make it easier to receive and convert digital assets, but the bigger challenge remains last-mile distribution: some recipients still want cash, bank credit, or mobile-money balances rather than a self-custodied token.

## The On-Ramp / Off-Ramp Problem

Stablecoins make the middle of the transaction cheap. The hard part is still the edge: cash in, cash out, and regulatory compliance. In countries with liquid crypto markets and permissive regulation, that problem is manageable. In countries with strict capital controls or thin local liquidity, the spread can consume most of the benefit.

This is improving. Local exchanges, fintech distribution partners, and mobile-money integrations all reduce friction. But this is still the main operational bottleneck. A remittance product is only as good as its cash-in and cash-out network.

Wise and similar fintechs already lowered remittance costs for many banked users. Stablecoins matter most where that improvement still has not arrived: corridors with weak banking interoperability, expensive cash-out, or users who are underbanked rather than fully banked.

## Remaining Barriers

Regulatory uncertainty remains a major obstacle. Many countries still treat stablecoin transfers as crypto activity subject to licensing, reporting, tax complexity, or outright restrictions. AML and sanctions compliance do not disappear just because settlement happens onchain.

Consumer trust is another issue. Remittance senders are often highly risk-sensitive. Self-custody can be unforgiving, so the products most likely to scale are custodial or semi-custodial apps that hide wallet complexity while preserving the cost and speed benefits of stablecoin rails.

The infrastructure is functional today. The limiting factors are local distribution, product design, and regulation.
