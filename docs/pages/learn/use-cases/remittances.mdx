# Remittances

Global remittance flows reached an estimated $860 billion in 2024, with $685 billion flowing to low- and middle-income countries. The average cost of sending $200 across borders is 6.4%, according to the World Bank. In sub-Saharan African corridors, fees regularly exceed 8%. A worker in Dubai sending $500 home to the Philippines pays $25-40 in fees and waits 1-3 days for the money to arrive.

These costs are not mysterious. They're the price of moving money through a chain of intermediaries — the sending agent, the sending bank, a correspondent bank (sometimes two), the receiving bank, and the cash-out agent. Each one takes a margin. Each one adds time.

## How Stablecoin Remittances Work

The sender converts local currency to a stablecoin (typically USDT or USDC) through an exchange, on-ramp app, or peer-to-peer market. The stablecoin is transferred directly to the recipient's wallet. The recipient either holds the stablecoin or converts it to local currency through a local exchange or cash-out partner.

The transfer itself costs under $0.01 on L2 networks like Base, Arbitrum, or Optimism. It settles in under a minute. The total cost to the user depends on the on-ramp and off-ramp — converting between fiat and stablecoins — which typically ranges from 0.5% to 2%. Even at the high end, that's less than half the cost of traditional remittance services in most corridors.

## Corridor-Specific Adoption

**US to Latin America.** This is the world's largest remittance corridor. Mexico alone received $64 billion in remittances in 2024. Services like Bitso in Mexico and Lemon in Argentina process stablecoin-based transfers that settle in minutes. In Argentina, where the peso lost over 50% of its value against the dollar in 2023, recipients often prefer to hold USDT rather than convert to local currency — the stablecoin serves simultaneously as a remittance rail and a savings instrument.

**Africa.** Intra-African remittances are notoriously expensive — the Nigeria-to-Ghana corridor averages 9-10% in fees. Mobile-money-to-crypto bridges are emerging. Yellow Card, operating across 20+ African countries, enables stablecoin purchases and transfers via mobile money. In Nigeria, peer-to-peer USDT trading volume on platforms like Paxful historically exceeded that of every country except the US and Russia.

**Southeast Asia.** The Philippines receives $38 billion annually in remittances, mostly from workers in the Middle East, the US, and East Asia. Coins.ph and similar platforms offer stablecoin-based receive options. The Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas has been relatively progressive on crypto regulation, which helps. The bigger challenge is last-mile distribution — getting value from a digital wallet to a recipient who may prefer cash.

## The On-Ramp / Off-Ramp Problem

Stablecoins make the middle of the transaction nearly free. The expensive part is the edges: converting fiat to stablecoins and back. In countries with liquid crypto markets and light-touch regulation, this is straightforward and cheap. In countries with strict capital controls or limited exchange infrastructure, the fiat conversion spread can eat most of the savings.

This is improving. Circle's partnership network for USDC, Tether's direct issuance relationships, and the growth of local exchanges in emerging markets are all compressing on-ramp/off-ramp costs. Mobile money integrations — connecting services like M-Pesa or GCash directly to stablecoin wallets — eliminate one conversion step entirely.

|--------|--------------------------|------|---------------------|
| Fee (on $500) | $25-45 (5-9%) | $5-15 (1-3%) | $1-10 (0.2-2%) |
| Speed | Minutes to 3 days | 1-2 days | Under 1 minute |
| Availability | Agent locations only | Bank account required | Internet connection |
| Minimum amount | Varies by corridor | ~$1 | No minimum |

Wise and similar fintech services already reduced remittance costs significantly for banked users. Stablecoins extend that cost reduction to unbanked and underbanked populations who have smartphone access but no bank account — roughly 1.4 billion adults globally.

## Remaining Barriers

Regulatory uncertainty remains the largest obstacle. Many countries classify stablecoin transfers as crypto transactions subject to capital gains tax, reporting requirements, or outright restrictions. Compliance with anti-money-laundering rules requires transaction monitoring, which is harder (though not impossible) on public blockchains.

Consumer trust is another factor. Remittance senders are often risk-averse with money they cannot afford to lose. Self-custody wallets require key management that is unforgiving of mistakes. Custodial solutions with familiar mobile interfaces — apps that look like Venmo, not MetaMask — are where most volume will flow.

The infrastructure is functional today. The user experience and regulatory framework are catching up.
