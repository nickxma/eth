# Microtransactions

Traditional payment rails have a floor cost. Between processor pricing, interchange, and fixed fees, very small payments are economically unattractive on cards and bank rails. That is why sub-dollar business models usually get bundled into subscriptions, ad-supported products, or prepaid balances instead of being billed per use.

The constraint is economic, not conceptual. Traditional payment systems were not built for fractions of a cent.

## L2 Economics Change the Math

On Ethereum L2 networks, transaction fees are often far below a dollar and can be fractions of a cent. That makes low-value payments much more viable than they are on card rails. But the threshold matters: cent-level and low-cent payments are now plausible; true sub-mill pricing often still requires batching, prepaid balances, or offchain netting.

This does not just make existing small payments cheaper. It makes some business models operationally possible in a way they were not before.

## API Monetization

Most APIs are sold through subscriptions or usage tiers. That model works, but it is imprecise. Low-volume users can overpay, and providers have to manage plans, overages, and billing infrastructure.

Pay-per-call pricing is cleaner. An API provider charges per request, and the developer pays only for what they consume. Stablecoin-based balances, prepaid contracts, or streaming payment mechanisms make that easier to implement without relying on a traditional billing intermediary.

Bandwidth marketplaces, compute-on-demand services, and metered data products are natural fits.

## Content Micropayments

The internet's advertising model won partly because micropayments never worked cleanly. Payment friction was higher than the value of the content being sold.

L2 stablecoins remove much of the cost barrier. A reader could pay a few cents to read an article, or a listener could pay per stream. The harder problem is still UX. Requiring a deliberate wallet action for every tiny purchase is worse than the business model it replaces.

The most plausible design is therefore not one transaction per click. It is a prefunded balance inside a browser, app, or wallet, with usage settled automatically in the background.

The Lightning Network on Bitcoin pioneered internet-native micropayments for some use cases. Stablecoins on Ethereum L2s offer a similar promise with dollar-denominated pricing, which is generally easier for commerce and API billing.

## Machine-to-Machine Payments

IoT and software agents generate economic activity that does not fit human approval flows. An EV could pay for charging, a sensor could sell data, or a compute worker could be paid for cycles delivered.

These transactions are often small, frequent, and automated. They require rails that are programmable, always available, and cheap enough that fees do not overwhelm the underlying value.

Smart contracts can encode the pricing logic: a device or service deposits stablecoins into a payment contract, and usage fees are deducted automatically based on metered consumption. No invoicing, no monthly reconciliation, no accounts receivable.

This area is still early. Many deployments are proofs of concept rather than scaled businesses. But the rail constraints that previously blocked the model are meaningfully lower than they used to be.

## Payment Channels and Streaming

For very high-frequency micropayments, even L2 fees add up if every event is a separate onchain transaction. That is why serious micropayment systems usually rely on some form of netting: state channels, prepaid balances, application-level ledgers, or periodic settlement.

The classic state-channel model is simple: two parties lock funds in a contract, exchange signed balance updates offchain, and settle the final state onchain later. One opening transaction, one closing transaction, many intermediate updates.

Streaming protocols such as Superfluid solve a related but different problem. They enable continuous onchain payment flows over time. That is useful for subscriptions and metered services, but it is not the same thing as a payment channel.

## Why This Matters

Micropayments do not just make small purchases cheaper. They enable pricing models that are structurally awkward on legacy rails: per-call billing, real-time revenue sharing, automated machine payments, and low-friction digital commerce.

The gap between "technically possible" and "widely adopted" is still wide. Wallet UX, demand aggregation, and product design all need to improve. But the payment-rail cost floor is no longer the main blocker.
